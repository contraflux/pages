Nozzle Optimization

My first project was to optimize our rocket nozzle. A nozzle consists of two sections, the converging section that funnels the exhaust gas to the throat while speeding it up, and then the diverging section that allows the gas to expand, further icnreasing its velocity. There are five main parameters that define a converging-diverging nozzle:

1. Converging half-angle: The angle between the converging wall and the axial direction
2. Diverging half-angle: The angle between the diverging wall and the axial direction
3. Entry area: The cross-sectional area of the nozzle at the entry
4. Throat area: The cross-sectional area of the nozzle at the throat
5. Exit area: The cross-sectional area of the nozzle at the exit

From our static hotfire test last year, the peak chamber pressure was around 590 PSI. From this pressure and Vieille's Law, I found our mass flow rate. From the mass flow rate, chamber pressure, and choked condition at the nozzle throat, I then calculated the required throat area. I also then calculated the optimal exit Mach number, and from that I then found the optimal expansion ratio, and thus exit area.

I found an optimal expansion ratio of 5.81, while our expansion ratio last year was 4.49. To verify my calculations, I ran a series of simulations using Ansys Fluent, and calculated the force for each.

From Table 1, you can see that for the 25-26 conical nozzle the exit pressure was closer to the optimum of 0 Pa, which lead to increased exit velocies. This lead to increased thrust and specific impulse, though the performance increases were less than what we had hoped for.

To verify that this marginal performance increase made sense, I approximated the inverse to the area-Mach relation and found a curve for thrust from expansion ratio.

Figure 3: Thrust in kilonewtons vs expansion ratio

From Figure 3 we can see a maximum right around 5.8 yielding around 6000 kN, but small increases or decreases in the expansion ratio hardly change the thrust. This graph backs up the small thrust change see in the CFD runs.

After this lackluster result, I continued research into optimizing for thrust output, and came acrossa a series of journal entries written by G.V.R. Rao describing a method he found for curving the diverging section of the nozzle to increase performance.

Figure 4: Sketch of the diverging section of a Rao nozzle.
"Approximation of Optimum Thrust Nozzle Contour," G.V.R. Rao, Jan. 4 1959.

He then approximated these ideal wall contours using a circular arc close to the throat and a parabola towards the exit. Using the expansion ratio and throat radius I found previously, I was able to use Rao's charts to design a new nozzle. I then ran the new nozzle in Fluent to characterize its performance.

From the results in Table 2, we can see that the Rao nozzle offers much larger increases in thrust and specific impulse, though the manufacturing complexity might outweigh the potential performance increases.

I then used CAD to create two models of our potential solid rocket nozzle for the 2025-26 season. Both had expansion ratios of 5.81, while one was a pure conical nozzle and the other was a Rao nozzle.
