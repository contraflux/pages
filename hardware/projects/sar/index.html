MIT Synethic Aperture Radar

Range-Time Intensity Plots

Over the summer of 2024, I had the opportunity to learn about and work with radars at MIT. The radar was a small ultra-wideband radar called the PulsON 440, and we communicated with it via Python scripts from a Raspberry Pi.

Figure 1: The PulsON 440 mounted below a Raspberry Pi. The two green triangles are log periodic antennae, one for transmitting and one for receiving.

The project consisted of a few phases, the first of which was writing the python program to interact with the PulsON 440. The data gained from the radar was then plotted on a range-time intensity plot, with range on the x-axis and time on the y-axis.

Figure 2: Range-time intensity plot dipicting an an object
that starts 60m away and moves to 50m over 3 seconds

Figure 3: Range-time intensity plot showing an object oscillating
between 10m and 4m with a period of 3.5 seconds

We noticed it could be difficult to make out small objects or objects at far distances due to the radar's limited power, so we also created delta range-time intensity plots, which subtract two adjacent pulses to find the differences between them. Along with making motion easier to see, these plots had the added benefit of reducing the large initial pulse detected by the receiving antenna as the radar is transmitting.

Time Domain Backprojection

First, I created CAD models of the three lower panels, designing them to contain parts we had already ordered, like the throttle quadrant and flap and trim panels. Each panel has a wood backing with a spraypainted acrylic cover. We then cut the wood on one of our mills and laser cut the acrylic.

Figure 1: Center panel wood backing on the mill

Figure 2: Center and right panel acrylic having just been spray painted

Figure 3: Initial assembly of center and right panels with trim, throttle quadrant, and flap controls

Once we had the panels done, I started creating a CAD model of the linked yokes mechanism. My plan was to use drawer slides for the pitch movement, and then link the two yokes togther to match roll control. I also designed both yokes to link to a center rod that would translate the control movements to a modified Honeycomb Alpha yoke to register control inputs.
